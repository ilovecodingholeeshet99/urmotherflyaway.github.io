Origins of Tennis

The racket sport traditionally named lawn tennis, invented in Birmingham, England, now commonly known simply as tennis, is the direct descendant of what is now denoted real tennis or royal tennis, which continues to be played today as a separate sport with more complex rules. Most rules of (lawn) tennis derive from this precursor and it is reasonable to see both sports as variations of the same game.

Did you Know?

Tennis is played on various surfaces, including grass, clay, and hard courts, each of which affects the style of play and strategy employed by players.

Tennis tournaments such as the Grand Slam events (Australian Open, French Open, Wimbledon, and US Open) attract players and fans from around the world.

Many original tennis courts remain, including courts at Oxford, Cambridge, Falkland Palace in Fife where Mary Queen of Scots regularly played, and Hampton Court Palace. Many of the French courts were decommissioned with the terror that accompanied the French Revolution.

Did you Know?

Tennis has origins dating back to 12th-century France. The term "tennis" comes from the French word "tenez," which means "take heed" or "receive."

The first known tennis courts were built in monasteries.

The modern form of tennis evolved from "real tennis" or "royal tennis."

The Medieval form of tennis is termed as real tennis, a game that evolved over three centuries from an earlier ball game played around the 12th century in France that involved hitting a ball with a bare hand and later with a glove. By the 16th century the glove had become a racquet, the game had moved to an enclosed playing area and the rules had stabilized. Real tennis spread in popularity throughout royalty in Europe, reaching its peak in the 16th century.

Did you Know?

Arthur Ashe became the first African-American male to win a Grand Slam title in 1968.

Billie Jean King defeated Bobby Riggs in the famous "Battle of the Sexes" match in 1973.

Bjorn Borg won six French Open titles and five Wimbledon titles during his career.

Tennis in the 1880s was dominated by the remarkable twin brothers William and Ernest Renshaw. William won the Wimbledon singles championship seven times, on three occasions defeating his brother in the final. Ernest was victorious once, and in partnership they won the doubles championship, first played at Oxford in 1879, seven times.

Did you Know?

Steffi Graf is the only player to achieve a Golden Slam, winning all four majors and the Olympic gold medal in 1988.

Martina Navratilova holds the record for most singles titles at Wimbledon with nine.

Andre Agassi is the only male player to achieve a career Grand Slam and an Olympic gold medal.
